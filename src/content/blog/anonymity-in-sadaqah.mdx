There is a hadith, narrated in Sahih al-Bukhari, in which the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, said that Sadaqah (voluntary charity) given in secret is better than that given openly. The scholars have argued about the exact weight of that hierarchy for centuries, but the principle underneath it is consistent across the tradition: the gift is between the giver and the receiver, and the rest of us are not in the conversation.

At mupla, we take that principle seriously. It shapes how we publish, how we thank, and how we list donors.

## What anonymity protects

Two things, mainly. The first is the dignity of the recipient. A pantry neighbor who receives groceries should not have their name in our annual report; that would make them a case study, not a neighbor. The second is the sincerity of the giver. A donor whose name is publicly celebrated has begun to receive social reward for the gift, and the social reward is not what the gift was for.

The scholars also note a third protection, less often spoken about: anonymity protects the giver from the quiet seduction of feeling generous. The moment you have been thanked publicly, the work of giving is over, and the work of feeling good about having given has begun. Sadaqah is supposed to resist that.

## The scholarly nuance

The hadith on secret giving is not a flat prohibition on public charity. Several of the Companions gave publicly in ways that the Prophet did not criticize, and the scholars of the four Sunni schools have spent centuries mapping the cases. The general rule is: the more public the gift, the more careful the giver must be about the niyyah, the intention, and the more exposed the gift is to riya, the spiritual disease of showing off.

In practice, the tradition distinguishes three kinds of giving. The first is Sadaqah that is best kept secret: the routine monthly gift, the gift to a person you do not know, the gift that funds a program no one will associate with you. The second is Sadaqah that can be public when the publicness serves the community: a public Zakat distribution that draws others to give, a scholarship announcement that inspires other students, a named room that lets a foundation name its benefactors. The third is Sadaqah that is corrupted by being public: the gift that is announced to elevate the giver, the gift that is used as a tax receipt at a dinner party, the gift that becomes a status symbol.

Our standing policy follows this distinction. We keep the routine anonymous. We celebrate the public when it serves the community. We refuse the corrupted case.

## How we handle donor names

In our annual report, we list donors only when the donor has explicitly opted in. The default is silent. If you give $25 a month and do not check the "list me in the report" box, you will not appear in the report. If you check the box, you will appear under whatever name you specify, first name and last initial only.

This is the standing policy, and it is the policy of our Zakat committee too, even though Zakat is fard (obligatory), not Sadaqah. The principle holds. To give Zakat on our [donate page](/donate), the form never asks for your name unless you want a tax receipt.

## What we ask of donors

Three things, in order of importance. The first is to give what you can, regularly, in a way that does not strain the household. The tradition is clear: a gift that hurts the giver is not a gift, it is a debt. The second is to give it in a way that protects the recipient's dignity: not as a public display, not as a named case study, not with a follow-up request for a photo. The third is to let the foundation do the thank-you in the form of a written, anonymous note, or not at all. Many of our donors ask us not to acknowledge the gift at all, and we honor that.

## Anonymity in our weekly programs

The principle is not just about money. Our Saturday food pantry is anonymous. We do not take names at the door. We do not photograph the families who come through. We do not publish a list of who received what. The groceries are on the table; the family takes what they need; the family leaves; the family is not on camera. The same is true of our refugee resettlement program, our Zakat distribution, and our senior's tea. The people we serve are not a case study. They are neighbors. Neighbors are not catalogued.

## The exception

There is one place we do put names. When a donor has asked us to, when the family they are honoring has asked us to, and when the gift is a public dedication, like a named scholarship or a built-out room in the community center. In those cases, we celebrate the gift openly, because that is what the gift was for.

For everything else, the tradition is clear, and our reporting follows it. If you have questions, the [FAQ page](/faq) covers the most common ones, or write to hello@mupla.org. To see how the donations move through our [programs](/programs), the programs page has the line items per program.
