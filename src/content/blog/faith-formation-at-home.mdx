Parents in our workshops often arrive with the same question, in slightly different forms: how do I raise faithful children in a world that pulls in every direction? They are tired. They are worried. They are reading parenting books that tell them the answer is a strict schedule, a careful curriculum, and a system of rewards.

This is a reflection on what we have heard back from families who have tried something simpler.

## The five habits

None of these are original. All of them are small. All of them have been tested across dozens of households in our community, and all of them have produced, over time, the thing parents say they want: kids who pray, who read, who are kind, who come back to the deen when they are old enough to leave.

**One, Fajr together once a week.** You will not get to Fajr every day as a family, and the parents who try often burn out by month three. Pick one morning. Saturday. Sunday. The day your spouse is off. Wake the kids, make wudu together, pray, eat breakfast. One morning a week, consistently, for a year, is the difference between a household that prays and one that does not.

**Two, a family Qur'an half-hour.** After dinner, or after Maghrib on a weekday, fifteen minutes of recitation and reflection. The same Surah for a week at a time. The kids memorize it without trying. The adults memorize it without trying. The household has a shared vocabulary.

**Three, du'a at the door.** When the kids leave the house, say a du'a with them at the door. When they come back, say one at the door. This is the Prophetic practice, and it costs nothing. It anchors the day.

**Four, weekly Sadaqah.** A jar on the kitchen counter. Every family member puts something in on Friday. You decide together where it goes: the neighbor who lost a job, the kid at school whose family is struggling, the [foundation's Zakat fund](/donate). The practice of giving is a habit, and habits are built in childhood.

**Five, one honest conversation a month.** No phones, no agenda, no lesson plan. Just sitting with your kid, at their level, and asking the real questions. How are you, really. What are you worried about. What do you want to be when you grow up. Once a month, the conversation happens. The rest of the time, the household is busy.

## What doesn't work

We have heard back, over the years, about a few approaches that did not work. We name them here, gently, because the families who tried them are not failures, and the approaches are not wrong in the abstract. They were wrong for the households that tried them.

A strict daily schedule that did not survive the first crisis. A long curriculum that took the parents' best hour and produced resentment. A reward system that turned prayer into a transaction. A punishment system that turned prayer into a chore. None of these are intrinsically wrong; they are wrong when the household is tired, and most households, most of the time, are tired.

The five habits above are designed for the tired household. None of them require the parents' best hour. None of them require a curriculum. None of them are a transaction. They are practices the household can keep up when the household is in a season of strain, and the practices are what survive the strain.

## A note on the parents' own faith practice

The hidden variable in all five habits is not the kids. It is the parents. The kids will follow whatever the parents do, not whatever the parents say. If the parents pray, the kids pray. If the parents give, the kids give. If the parents put the phone down for one honest conversation a month, the kids will, too.

This is sometimes a hard thing to hear, because the parents are often the ones who came to the workshop asking how to fix the kids. The kids are fine. The kids are mirrors. The workshop is also a workshop for the parents' own practice, and the parents' own practice is the thing that changes the household.

We have learned to be more direct about this. The first session of the next parenting workshop will name this directly. The five habits are for the parents. The kids will follow.

## What we hear from the parents

After a year of trying the five habits, the parents who stick with them report three things. The first is that the household is calmer. The Fajr morning becomes the day the household starts together, and the rest of the day has a different shape. The second is that the kids are kinder to each other. The du'a at the door and the weekly Sadaqah jar teach the kids that other people are part of the household's attention, and the kindness follows. The third is that the parents' own faith practice is stronger. The habits are habits for the parents, and the parents are the ones who change.
