Last weekend, fifteen parents gathered at the community center for our spring parenting workshop — "Faith, Screens, and the Long Game." It was a smaller group than usual. The conversation was unhurried, the questions honest, and we left with a clearer picture of where the next workshop should aim.

## What we covered

The evening ran two hours, broken into three short sessions with breaks for tea and snacks in between:

1. **Faith as a home practice.** Three parents opened up about what rituals they currently keep in their homes — anything from a daily Fajr that everyone rises for, to a weekly family Qur'an half-hour after dinner. We did not propose a "right" pattern; we shared what was working, what had stopped working, and what each family wanted to revisit.
2. **Screens, attention, and the household rule.** The honest conversation. We talked about screen-time for kids seven and up, the way family meals shift when a phone is on the table, and parent guilt — a lot of parent guilt. We sketched a few practical rules that families could adapt instead of adopt: devices out of bedrooms at night, no phones at the dinner table, a shared family charging station in the living room.
3. **The long game.** A short reflection on how the parenting literature tends to focus on the seasons that test them most — toddler defiance, teenage rebellion — and how the harder, slower work is being a steady, present adult in a child's day for the eighteen or twenty years that they live under your roof. The traditional Prophetic du'a we recited at the end (Rabbi habli mina-s-salihin) lands here more than anywhere: the request is for righteous children *who will outlive you*, not for short-term wins.

## What we heard back

We sent a short feedback form. A few themes came back:

> "It helped that no one was telling us what to do — we were just talking about what we already try to do, and the workshop helped us name it."
>
> "I realized my screen 'rules' had collapsed quietly over the last year. Time to restart."
>
> "Would love one of these just for moms / just for dads — fewer people, more honest."

We are listening. The fall workshop will likely repeat the format with the requested splits.
