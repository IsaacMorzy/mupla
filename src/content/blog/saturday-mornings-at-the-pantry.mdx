Every Saturday at 8 a.m., a small crew unlocks the mupla community center. By 8:15, the folding tables are set up. By 8:30, the first boxes of halal staples arrive from the regional food bank. By 9, the volunteers, usually eight to twelve of them, are pairing up at assembly-line stations along the back wall.

This is the food pantry in operation. It looks mundane on paper. It looks like a handful of people packing grocery boxes. In practice, it is the most consistent weekly service we run, and the most essential.

## What the work actually is

Each week, our Saturday crew packs **roughly 60 grocery boxes**, one per family on the recipient list. A typical box holds rice, flour, oil, halal proteins, fresh produce when available, diapers if a household has young children, and shelf-stable staples to round out the week. The boxes are sorted by family size, anonymized, and either delivered by carpool or picked up between 11 and 2.

In a typical month, that means **240 boxes** going out the door. Across a year, roughly **2,800**. The numbers are not what makes it important; what makes it important is that everyone who walks through our doors on Saturday sees, in person, the family that is going to eat from what they packed.

## Why we do it with volunteers

We could pay a part-time staff to do this work. We choose not to. A volunteer-driven pantry is slower, less efficient, and harder to scale. But it is also a way of putting the people doing the serving in direct contact with the people being served, and that is the point. Service, as our tradition teaches, is not a thing one group performs for another. It is the daily practice of recognizing that your neighbor's need is your responsibility.

## How to help

We are short-handed in two specific ways: drivers who can spend two hours on Saturday morning delivering boxes along established routes, and people willing to commit to a recurring pantry shift. If either applies, our [get involved page](/get-involved) has sign-ups, or you can email volunteer@mupla.org directly.
